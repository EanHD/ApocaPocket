steps:
- 'WOOD SELECTION — Best pairings (spindle and fireboard should be same wood or similar hardness): Willow-on-willow (excellent, easiest), cottonwood-on-cottonwood (excellent), cedar-on-cedar (good), basswood-on-basswood (good), poplar-on-poplar (good), aspen-on-aspen (good), yucca-on-yucca (excellent in desert). Spindle should be slightly harder than fireboard. Avoid hard woods (oak, maple, hickory, ash) — they require extreme effort and rarely succeed.'
- 'MOISTURE TEST: wood must be completely dry. Snap test: should break with sharp crack, not bend. Surface should be bone-dry to touch. Ideal: standing dead wood (still upright but dead 1+ years), found above ground, no bark rot. Avoid: ground wood, green wood, punky/rotten wood.'
- 'FIREBOARD DIMENSIONS: flat piece of dry wood, 12 inches long × 3-4 inches wide × ½-¾ inch thick (too thin = breaks, too thick = can''t pin down). Carve a small starter depression (⅜" diameter, ⅛" deep) near one edge for the spindle, at least 1" from edge. After first attempt, cut a V-notch from the edge into the center of the depression (45-60° angle, pie slice shape cutting 1/8 into the socket) — this collects hot dust to form the ember.'
- 'SPINDLE DIMENSIONS: straight, dry stick, thumb-width (¾ inch diameter), 12-18 inches long. Too thin = breaks under pressure, too thick = hard to spin. Carve sharp point (30° angle) on top end (goes in handhold), and blunt/rounded end (60° angle dome) on bottom (goes in fireboard). The blunt end creates more friction surface area. Remove all bark. Should be perfectly straight — even slight curve causes wobble and failure.'
- 'BOW DIMENSIONS: sturdy curved stick, arm-length (24-30 inches), thumb-thick or slightly thicker. Natural curve helps but straight works. String with paracord (preferred), bootlace, natural cordage, or twisted plant fiber (36-40" string length). Tie with fixed knot at one end, adjustable knot at other to control tension. Tension should be tight enough that spindle doesn''t slip but loose enough to wrap spindle once. Too tight = can''t wrap spindle.'
- 'HANDHOLD: hardwood piece (3"×3"×1"), stone with natural depression, coconut shell, or bone that fits comfortably in your palm. Carve or find a socket depression (½" wide, ⅜" deep) in the center. CRITICAL: Lubricate the top socket with earwax, pine resin, soap, animal fat, or lip balm to reduce friction (you want friction only at the BOTTOM on fireboard). Without lube, handhold will smoke and burn.'
- 'SETUP POSITION: kneel with left foot forward (if right-handed), right knee on ground. Place fireboard under your left foot, with socket hole near the arch of your foot. Pin fireboard firmly — it must not move. Place small flat bark or wood chip under V-notch to catch ember dust (catches falling ember). Tinder bundle should be within arm''s reach.'
- 'BOW LOADING: Wrap bow string ONCE around spindle (string in front, spindle behind). The string should cross over itself on the spindle, creating friction. Place spindle''s blunt end in fireboard socket. Place handhold on top of spindle''s pointed end. Your bow hand is extended, handhold hand is directly above the spindle over your forward foot.'
- 'BODY MECHANICS: lock your handhold arm against your left shin (creates solid pressure). Your left arm should be braced against your left leg forming a stable triangle. Bow arm moves freely in horizontal plane. Keep spindle vertical (perpendicular to fireboard) — leaning spindle causes it to "walk" and pop out.'
- 'TECHNIQUE PHASE 1 (warm-up): start with slow, smooth, full-length bow strokes (90% of bow length) to warm the wood and create socket wear pattern. Apply moderate downward pressure (10-15 lbs). Continue for 15-20 seconds. You should see light smoke and fine dust starting to form. This breaks in the socket.'
- 'TECHNIQUE PHASE 2 (coal formation): Increase to MAXIMUM speed and apply heavy downward pressure (20-30 lbs). Keep full-length strokes — short jerky strokes waste energy. Your bow arm should be moving fast enough that it blurs. When thick white/brown smoke appears continuously (30-45 seconds total), maintain maximum effort for 10-15 MORE seconds. Speed + pressure = heat. Aim for 60-80 strokes per minute at maximum phase.'
- 'EMBER FORMATION: you''ll see dark brown dust accumulating in the V-notch. When you see a glowing coal forming or smell distinct burning smell (like smoldering leaves), complete 5-10 more fast strokes, then STOP. Do not disturb it immediately. Gently lift the spindle, then the fireboard away carefully. The ember should be smoking on its own — a self-sustaining dark red/brown coal about the size of a pea. It may take 5-10 seconds after stopping to fully form.'
- 'EMBER VERIFICATION: ember should produce continuous smoke trail. If smoke stops after 5 seconds, you don''t have a true ember — coal needs more heat. Gently blow on it (1-2 light breaths) to see if it glows brighter. True ember will glow orange-red when blown on.'
- 'TRANSFER TO TINDER: use bark chip to carefully tip the ember (and all the dust around it) into the center of your prepared tinder bundle. Tinder bundle structure: fine dry grass/bark shavings in center, progressively coarser material outside, bird nest shape (8-10" diameter). Gently fold tinder around the ember creating contact but allowing oxygen. Position bundle so ember is in bottom center.'
- 'IGNITION: blow with steady, gentle breaths (2-3 seconds per breath) at the base where ember sits. As smoke increases, blow harder and more continuously. Cup bundle loosely in hands to concentrate heat. Smoke will transition from white (water vapor) to thick (ember burning tinder) to flame. This takes 30-60 seconds typically. When flames appear, place immediately into prepared fire lay.'
- 'TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE: Black dust/no ember = too much pressure, not enough speed (speed up). Brown dust/no ember = increase pressure. Dust but no smoke = wood too damp or dusty (try new wood). String slipping on spindle = tighten bow tension OR roughen spindle with knife cuts OR add pine resin/rosin to string. Spindle popping out = not vertical, or socket too shallow. Handhold smoking = add lubricant. Ember dies after transfer = tinder too coarse (need finer center material) or blowing too hard initially.'
- 'PRACTICE BENCHMARKS: 1) First success (any ember): 5-10 attempts typical. 2) Consistent success (80%+ rate): 20-30 practice sessions. 3) Speed milestone: sub-3-minute ember (from setup to ember) indicates mastery. 4) Weather challenge: achieve ember in damp/cold conditions with emergency tinder.'

# Bow Drill Fire Starting — Complete Guide

The bow drill is the most reliable primitive friction fire method. Success depends on wood selection, proper dimensions, correct technique, and prepared tinder. Most failures come from wrong wood, wet materials, or insufficient speed/pressure. Success rate exceeds 80% with practice and proper materials.

## Quick Reference

**Component Dimensions:**
- Fireboard: 12" × 3-4" × ½-¾"
- Spindle: ¾" diameter × 12-18" long
- Bow: 24-30" long, thumb-thick
- Handhold: 3" × 3" × 1" with socket

**Critical Success Factors:**
1. Bone-dry wood (60% of success)
2. Correct wood pairing (20%)
3. Proper technique (15%)
4. Good tinder (5%)

**Timing Milestones:**
- Warmup phase: 15-20 seconds
- Coal formation: 30-45 seconds total
- Ember to flame: 30-60 seconds
- Total time (experienced): 2-5 minutes

**Common Wood Pairings (Best to Good):**
1. Willow-on-willow (easiest)
2. Cottonwood-on-cottonwood
3. Cedar-on-cedar
4. Yucca-on-yucca (desert)
5. Basswood-on-basswood
6. Poplar-on-poplar

## Friction Physics

**Heat generation formula:** Heat = Pressure × Speed × Time  
**Target friction temperature:** 700-800°F (371-427°C)  
**Stroke rate at max effort:** 60-80 per minute  
**Downward pressure:** 20-30 lbs at coal formation phase
