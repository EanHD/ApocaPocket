steps:
- 'TINDER PREPARATION (critical — this is where most people fail): you need material that catches spark and holds flame. Best natural tinders: birch bark (thin papery outer bark, strips 1-2" wide), cedar bark (shredded to cotton consistency, handful-sized bundle), fatwood shavings (resin-saturated pine, pencil shavings thickness), cattail fluff (2-3 tablespoons), dried grass (crumbled fine, golf ball sized), pine needles (dried, crushed to break waxy coating), milkweed fluff (pinch-sized), thistle down.'
- 'Prepare a tinder bundle: baseball-sized nest (8-10" diameter) with the finest, driest material in the center, slightly coarser material around the outside. The spark must land on the finest material. This is a bird''s nest shape — hollow center 3-4" wide. Should be loosely packed to allow oxygen flow.'
- 'ENHANCED TINDERS (carry these): cotton balls with petroleum jelly (coat thoroughly — burns 3-5 minutes, prepare 10-20 per trip), dryer lint (stored in waterproof bag), waxed cardboard (corrugated works best, 2x2" pieces), jute twine (pulled apart into fine fibers, 6" length), charcloth (see char cloth entry). These are near-guaranteed to catch ferro rod sparks.'
- 'TECHNIQUE — Lock and scrape method (most reliable): hold the ferro rod stationary pressed against your tinder or positioned 2-3 inches above it. Place the spine of your knife (or dedicated striker) on the rod at midpoint. Pull the ROD BACKWARD 4-6 inches while pressing the striker forward firmly. Apply 10-15 lbs pressure. This keeps the striker over the tinder and doesn''t disturb the bundle.'
- 'Alternative: push method — hold rod steady with tip near tinder, push striker firmly along the rod toward the tinder with quick 6" stroke. Speed matters — fast scrape = hotter sparks. Faster but risks scattering tinder. Best for prepared tinders that won''t blow apart.'
- 'ANGLE: striker should meet the rod at 30-45°. Too shallow (<20°) = shaving off metal without sparking. Too steep (>60°) = sparks fly upward not forward. The sweet spot sends a shower of sparks (10-20 bright sparks per strike) directly into the tinder, 2-3 inches away.'
- 'SPARK GENERATION: use full-length strokes (4-6 inches minimum) with firm pressure. Short hesitant strokes waste material. Each strike removes ~0.1mm of rod material. A quality strike should produce bright white-hot sparks visible in daylight. If sparks are dull orange, increase speed or check striker sharpness.'
- 'When tinder catches (you''ll see a glowing ember or small flame): gently blow with steady breaths (2-3 second exhales) to build it. Cup hands around bundle to protect from wind. Place into your pre-built fire lay (tinder → kindling → fuel, already assembled BEFORE you strike). Fire lay should be arm''s length away from tinder prep area.'
- 'WET CONDITIONS: ferro rods work when wet — wipe dry on your shirt first. The challenge is dry tinder. Carry prepared tinder (petroleum jelly cotton balls) in a waterproof container (film canister or small Nalgene). In the field, look inside dead standing trees (standing dead wood stays drier), under bark slabs, in the center of dead grass clumps, under dense conifer branches, or scrape wood shavings from dry interior of logs.'
- 'MAINTENANCE: ferro rods don''t expire. Store dry. If oxidation develops (white powder on surface), scrape it off with knife edge. A 6-inch rod (3/8" diameter) provides 10,000-20,000 strikes. Carry a backup (3" mini rod on keychain). Replace when rod worn down to 2" or diameter reduced by half.'
- 'PRACTICE DRILL: achieve consistent ignition in under 30 seconds with natural tinder before relying on ferro rod in emergency. Practice in wet conditions. Time yourself: prepared tinder should ignite in 1-3 strikes; natural tinder in 5-10 strikes once mastered.'

# Ferro Rod and Tinder Preparation

A ferrocerium rod (ferro rod) is the most reliable fire starter for field use — works when wet, at altitude, in wind, and produces 3000°C sparks. Success depends entirely on tinder preparation. The rod lasts thousands of strikes.

## Quick Reference

**Success Rate Factors:**
- Tinder preparation: 70% of success
- Striker technique: 20%
- Rod quality: 10%

**Spark Temperature:** 3000°C (5432°F)
**Lifespan:** 10,000-20,000 strikes (6" rod)
**Effective Range:** 2-3 inches
**Ideal Stroke Length:** 4-6 inches
**Optimal Angle:** 30-45°
**Success Time:** 30 seconds or less (with practice)

## Tinder Bundle Construction

Create a bird's nest structure:
- **Outer layer:** Coarse dry grass/bark strips (finger-thick)
- **Middle layer:** Medium fibers (pencil-thick)
- **Inner core:** Finest material (hair-thin fibers)
- **Total size:** Baseball (8-10" diameter)
- **Center depression:** 3-4" wide for spark landing zone
- **Density:** Loosely packed — must allow oxygen flow
