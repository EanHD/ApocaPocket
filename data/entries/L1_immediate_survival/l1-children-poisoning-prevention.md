# Childproofing and Poisoning Prevention

## Overview

**Poisoning is a leading cause of injury in children under 6 years old.** Curious toddlers put everything in their mouths. Older children imitate adults (taking pills, drinking from bottles). In survival scenarios, increased stress + non-childproofed environments = higher poisoning risk.

**Statistics (normal times):**
- 2+ million poison exposures annually in US (children <6 = 50%)
- Most common: Medications, cleaning products, plants, personal care items
- **90% preventable** through proper storage and supervision

**In disasters:** Risk INCREASES
- Medications stored improperly (out of secure location)
- Cleaning agents (bleach, disinfectants) left accessible
- Gasoline, fuels, chemicals (for generators, heating)
- Plants (foraging mistakes, toxic lookalikes)

---

## Age-Specific Risks

### Infants (0-12 months)
**Mobility:** Rolling, crawling (by 6-8 months)  
**Risk:** Low (limited mobility, adult supervision high)  
**Hazards:**
- Medications within reach when changing diaper
- Cleaning products under sink (when crawling)
- Small objects (choking > poisoning at this age)

---

### Toddlers (1-3 years) ⚠️ HIGHEST RISK AGE
**Mobility:** Walking, climbing, opening cabinets  
**Cognition:** Extremely curious, no danger awareness, imitates adults  
**Risk:** **EXTREME** (peak age for poisoning)  
**Hazards:**
- Climbs to reach counters, tables
- Opens "childproof" caps (with enough time/frustration)
- Drinks from ANY container (doesn't read labels)
- Eats plants, berries, mushrooms (colorful = attractive)

**Critical period:** 18-36 months (maximum mobility + zero judgment)

---

### Preschool (3-5 years)
**Cognition:** Understands "no" but impulsive, still imitates  
**Risk:** Moderate to high  
**Hazards:**
- Vitamins (look like candy)
- Essential oils (smell good, taste terrible, toxic)
- Nail polish remover, hand sanitizer (alcohol)

---

### School Age (6-12 years)
**Cognition:** Better judgment, can read labels  
**Risk:** Low (unless intentional experimentation)  
**Hazards:**
- Intentional misuse (getting high, dares)
- Medication errors (taking own medicine incorrectly)

## Common Household Poisons (Survival Scenarios)

### Medications (TOP CAUSE)

**High-risk medications (even 1-2 pills can harm children):**

| Medication | Child Danger | Symptoms if Ingested |
|------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Opioids (hydrocodone, oxycodone) | EXTREME | Drowsiness, slow breathing, coma, death |
| Blood pressure pills (clonidine, beta-blockers) | EXTREME | Low blood pressure, slow heart rate, seizures |
| Diabetes medications (glipizide, metformin) | HIGH | Dangerously low blood sugar, seizures |
| Antidepressants (tricyclics) | EXTREME | Seizures, heart arrhythmias, coma |
| Muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine) | HIGH | Drowsiness, confusion, seizures |
| Iron supplements | EXTREME | Vomiting blood, liver failure (delayed 12-48 hours) |

**Storage rules:**
- ✅ HIGH shelf or locked box (above adult eye level, locked medication safe)
- ✅ Original childproof containers (never transfer to pill organizers)
- ✅ Purse/bag stored high (visitors' medications often forgotten in bags)
- ❌ NEVER on counters, nightstands, or in "childproof" bottles alone (kids defeat them)

---

### Cleaning Products

**Most dangerous:**
- **Bleach:** Corrosive (burns throat, stomach)
- **Drain cleaner (lye):** Extreme corrosive (can perforate esophagus)
- **Toilet bowl cleaner:** Acids or bleach (corrosive)
- **Dishwasher detergent pods:** Concentrated, attractive (looks like candy)

**Storage rules:**
- ✅ HIGH locked cabinet (not under sink)
- ✅ Original containers with labels intact
- ✅ Separate from food storage
- ❌ NEVER in food containers (soda bottle with bleach = deadly mistake)

---

### Fuels and Automotive Products

**Survival scenarios often require:**
- Gasoline (generators, vehicles)
- Kerosene (heaters)
- Lighter fluid, lamp oil
- Antifreeze (sweet taste, DEADLY)

**Storage rules:**
- ✅ Outside living area (garage, shed, outdoor storage)
- ✅ Locked shed or high shelf (gasoline vapors explode - ventilation critical)
- ✅ Color-coded and labeled (red = gasoline, blue = kerosene, green = antifreeze)
- ❌ NEVER in reach of children, NEVER in drink containers

---

### Plants (Indoor and Outdoor)

**Common toxic houseplants:**
- **Dieffenbachia (Dumb Cane):** Mouth/throat swelling, drooling
- **Philodendron:** Mouth burning, vomiting
- **Pothos (Devil's Ivy):** Mouth irritation, vomiting
- **Peace Lily:** Mouth/throat irritation

**Outdoor toxic plants (urban/suburban):**
- **Pokeweed:** Berries attractive (purple-black), cause vomiting, seizures
- **Yew (Taxus):** Red berries, needles toxic (heart arrhythmias)
- **Azalea, Rhododendron:** Leaves, flowers toxic (vomiting, coma)
- **Oleander:** Extremely toxic (one leaf can kill) - heart arrhythmias

**Storage rules:**
- ✅ Keep toxic plants high (hanging baskets) or remove entirely
- ✅ Teach children "never eat plants without asking adult"
- ✅ Know plant names (take photo to poison control if ingestion)

---

### Personal Care Products

**Attractive to children (smell, taste, color):**
- **Mouthwash:** High alcohol content (like drinking hard liquor)
- **Hand sanitizer:** 60-95% alcohol (intoxication, coma)
- **Essential oils:** Concentrated (eucalyptus, tea tree = seizures)
- **Nail polish remover (acetone):** Drowsiness, coma
- **Hair dye, perm solution:** Corrosive

**Storage rules:**
- ✅ HIGH bathroom cabinet (not under sink)
- ✅ Locked if alcohol-based (teenagers may seek intoxication)

## Childproofing Strategies (Layered Defense)

### Layer 1: Remove or Lock Away (BEST)

**Principle:** If child cannot access it, they cannot be poisoned by it.

**Implementation:**
1. **High storage:** Above 5 feet (adult eye level or higher)
2. **Locked cabinets:** Key or combination lock (not just latch)
3. **Remove entirely:** If you don't need it, get rid of it (fewer hazards)

---

### Layer 2: Childproof Packaging (SECOND LINE)

**Childproof caps:**
- Delay access (10-30 seconds for adult, minutes for child)
- NOT foolproof (persistent toddler can eventually open)
- **Re-lock after each use** (many adults leave caps loose)

**Blister packs:**
- Better than bottles (each pill individually wrapped)
- Slows ingestion (child must open multiple blisters)

**Child-resistant latches:**
- Cabinet latches (magnetic, slide locks)
- Toilet locks (children drink from toilet, toxic if cleaner added)

---

### Layer 3: Supervision (CRITICAL)

**"Eyes on" time:**
- Toddlers (1-3 years): Constant visual supervision (check every 2-3 minutes if in different room)
- If you must leave room: Bring child with you OR place in safe enclosed area (playpen, gated room)

**High-risk times:**
- Visiting others' homes (not childproofed)
- Moving/unpacking (items out of storage)
- Stressed/distracted adults (more likely to forget to lock cabinets)

---

### Layer 4: Education (LONG-TERM)

**Age-appropriate teaching:**

**18 months - 3 years:**
- "Yucky, no touch" (simple, clear)
- Redirect to safe activity (don't just say no, offer alternative)

**3-5 years:**
- "This is medicine, only mommy/daddy give you" (distinguish safe from unsafe)
- Role-play (practice saying no to strangers offering candy/pills)

**6+ years:**
- Read labels together (teach poison symbols ☠️)
- Explain WHY (medicine helps when sick, but wrong amount hurts)
- Teach them to ask adult before eating ANYTHING unknown

## Poisoning Prevention Checklist (Room-by-Room)

### Kitchen

- [ ] Medications stored HIGH and LOCKED (not in purse on counter)
- [ ] Cleaning products locked (not under sink)
- [ ] Dish soap pods in locked cabinet (attractive, dangerous)
- [ ] Trash can with lid or locked (discarded medications, spoiled food)
- [ ] Alcoholic beverages locked or high shelf

### Bathroom

- [ ] Medications locked (prescription + over-the-counter)
- [ ] Mouthwash, hand sanitizer HIGH (alcohol content)
- [ ] Toilet locked or cleaner stored separately (not in bowl or tank)
- [ ] Cosmetics, nail polish, remover HIGH
- [ ] Razors, tweezers locked (not poison but safety hazard)

### Bedroom

- [ ] Adult medications NOT on nightstand (purse, pockets emptied)
- [ ] Cosmetics, perfumes HIGH
- [ ] Laundry detergent pods locked (if laundry in bedroom)

### Garage / Utility Area

- [ ] Gasoline, kerosene, fuels LOCKED and outside living area
- [ ] Antifreeze locked (sweet taste attracts children AND pets)
- [ ] Pesticides, herbicides, rat poison LOCKED
- [ ] Tools with chemicals (paint thinner, solvents) LOCKED
- [ ] Car battery, cleaning fluids secured

### Living Areas

- [ ] Houseplants HIGH or removed (if toxic)
- [ ] Visitors' purses HIGH (often contain medications)
- [ ] Alcoholic drinks not left out (even small amounts toxic to kids)
- [ ] Fireplace/wood stove fuels locked (lighter fluid, starter)

## If Poisoning Occurs (Emergency Response)

### Immediate Steps

**1. Stay Calm (0-30 seconds)**
- Panic makes you forget critical steps
- Breathe deeply, focus on child

**2. Remove Source (30 seconds - 1 minute)**
- Take away container, plant, medication
- Wipe any residue from child's mouth (with finger or cloth)
- **DO NOT induce vomiting unless poison control instructs** (vomiting can worsen corrosive burns)

**3. Assess Child (1-2 minutes)**
- Is child conscious? Breathing? Alert?
- Visible burns on lips, mouth, or skin?
- Unusual drowsiness, confusion, or seizures?

**4. Call Poison Control (immediately)**
- **US:** 1-800-222-1222 (24/7, free, expert toxicologists)
- **Have ready:** Child's age, weight, substance ingested, amount, time since ingestion

---

### Poison Control Information You'll Need

**About the child:**
- Age and weight (affects toxicity)
- Symptoms (drowsy, vomiting, alert)

**About the poison:**
- Name (exact product name, medication name, plant name)
- Amount (number of pills, volume of liquid, size of plant part)
- Time (how long ago - critical for treatment window)

**Bring container/plant to phone** (read label to poison control)

---

### When to Call 911 (Immediate Life Threat)

**Call 911 INSTEAD OF or IN ADDITION TO poison control if:**
- Child is unconscious or difficult to wake
- Seizures (convulsions, shaking)
- Difficulty breathing (gasping, blue lips)
- Severe burns on lips, mouth, throat (corrosive ingestion)
- Known toxic substance (antifreeze, drain cleaner, large medication dose)

**Poison control can stay on line while 911 is en route** (call both if in doubt)

---

### What Poison Control Will Ask You

**Do NOT do before calling:**
- ❌ Induce vomiting (can worsen injury)
- ❌ Give milk (myth - does not neutralize most poisons)
- ❌ Wait to "see what happens" (treatment window may close)

**Poison control will instruct:**
- ✅ Monitor for symptoms (they'll tell you what to watch for)
- ✅ Give activated charcoal (IF they instruct and you have it)
- ✅ Go to ER (if severe ingestion)
- ✅ Home observation (if minor, non-toxic amount)

---

## Activated Charcoal (Advanced Preparation)

### What It Is
- Black powder (binds toxins in stomach)
- Prevents absorption into bloodstream
- **Effective if given within 1 hour of ingestion**

### When to Use
- **ONLY if poison control instructs**
- Not effective for: alcohol, acids/bases (corrosives), iron, lithium

### Dosing (Poison Control will provide exact dose)
- **1 gram per kg body weight** (typical dose)
- Example: 30 lb child (13.6 kg) = 13-14 grams charcoal
- Mixed with water (slurry consistency)

### How to Stock
- Buy: Activated charcoal powder or capsules (health food stores, pharmacy)
- Shelf life: Indefinite if kept dry
- Amount: 50-100 grams on hand (enough for 1-2 doses)

**DO NOT use without poison control guidance** (can cause aspiration if child vomits)

---

## Common Poisoning Myths (DANGEROUS)

| ❌ Myth | ✓ Truth |
|---------|---------|
| Induce vomiting with ipecac syrup | NO - Can cause aspiration, worsen corrosive burns. Ipecac no longer recommended. |
| Give milk to neutralize poison | NO - Milk does not neutralize most poisons, may speed absorption |
| Wait and see if symptoms develop | NO - Many poisons have delayed effects (iron, acetaminophen), early treatment critical |
| Burnt toast absorbs poison | NO - Not the same as activated charcoal (which must be medical-grade) |
| Childproof caps are foolproof | NO - Delay but don't prevent (toddlers can open with persistence) |

## Special Situations

### Visiting Non-Childproofed Homes

**Grandparents, friends, relatives:**
- Medications often on counters, nightstands
- Cleaning products under sinks (easy access)
- Plants, decorations within reach

**Strategy:**
- Ask host to childproof before visit (or do it yourself with permission)
- Bring childproofing supplies (cabinet latches, cord shorteners)
- Supervise constantly (don't rely on others' judgment of "safe")

---

### Survival Scenarios (Increased Risk)

**Grid-down, disasters:**
- Medications out of secure storage (frequently used)
- Fuels stored indoors (heating, cooking)
- Cleaning agents for sanitation (bleach, disinfectants)
- Foraging mistakes (poisonous plant ingestion)

**Mitigation:**
- Maintain locked storage even in emergencies
- Educate older children (they can help supervise younger)
- Mark all containers clearly (even if you "know what it is")

---

## Prevention for Specific Age Groups

### Infants (0-12 months)

**Primary strategy:** Supervision (can't childproof their mouths)

**Key actions:**
- Keep floor clear of small objects (choking > poisoning risk)
- Never leave medications on changing table
- Store diaper creams, ointments HIGH (zinc oxide toxic in large amounts)

---

### Toddlers (1-3 years)

**Primary strategy:** Lock everything (peak risk age)

**Key actions:**
- Assume they can climb anything (refrigerator, counters, furniture)
- Childproof caps + high storage + locked cabinets (triple layer)
- Immediate cleanup (don't leave pills on counter "for just a minute")

---

### Preschool (3-5 years)

**Primary strategy:** Lock + educate

**Key actions:**
- Teach them to ask before eating anything
- Explain "medicine is not candy"
- Practice scenarios ("What do you do if you find pills on floor?" - "Tell an adult, don't touch")

---

### School Age (6-12 years)

**Primary strategy:** Education + monitoring

**Key actions:**
- Teach them to read labels (poison symbols, warnings)
- Explain dosing (why taking more medicine doesn't help faster)
- Monitor for intentional misuse (huffing, experimenting)

---

## Long-Term Poisoning Risks (Chronic Exposure)

### Lead (Old Paint, Water)

**Sources:**
- Pre-1978 homes (lead paint chips, dust)
- Old plumbing (lead pipes, solder)

**Effects:**
- Permanent brain damage (lower IQ, learning disabilities)
- No safe level (even small amounts harmful)

**Prevention:**
- Test paint in old homes (lead test kits, $10-20)
- Filter water (lead-removing filter if old pipes)
- Wash hands frequently (especially before eating)

---

### Pesticides (Agricultural Areas, Lawns)

**Sources:**
- Sprayed lawns, gardens
- Agricultural drift (near farms)

**Effects:**
- Cumulative exposure (builds up over time)
- Neurological effects, increased cancer risk

**Prevention:**
- Avoid playing on recently sprayed lawns (24-48 hours)
- Wash produce thoroughly
- Remove shoes at door (pesticide residue on soles)
