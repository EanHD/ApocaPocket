# Large Game Shot Placement - Ethical Kills

**üéØ GOAL**: One shot, instant death, no suffering, no tracking, no wounded animal escaping.

**ETHICS**: If you shoot, you owe the animal a quick, clean death. Poor shot placement = prolonged suffering.

---

## Why Shot Placement Matters

**Consequences of Poor Shots:**
1. **Animal suffering**: Hours or days of pain before death
2. **Lost game**: Wounded animal escapes, meat wasted
3. **Dangerous tracking**: Wounded animals can charge (moose, bear)
4. **Legal/ethical issues**: Wounding without recovery is illegal in most jurisdictions
5. **Personal trauma**: Witnessing prolonged animal death

**Goal Statistics:**
- **Instant kill**: Death within 10 seconds (heart/spine/brain shots)
- **Acceptable**: Death within 1-2 minutes, minimal tracking (<50 yards)
- **Poor**: Tracking >100 yards, prolonged suffering

---

## ü´Ä Vital Organs & Kill Zones

### Priority Ranking (Instant Kill Likelihood)

**1. Heart/Lung Combo (RECOMMENDED)**
- **Location**: Behind front shoulder, lower 1/3 of body
- **Size**: 8-12 inch diameter circle (large target)
- **Result**: Massive blood loss, death in 10-30 seconds
- **Tracking**: 0-50 yards (usually drops within sight)
- **Shot angle**: Broadside (best) or quartering away

**2. Double Lung**
- **Location**: Behind shoulder, center chest cavity
- **Size**: 10-15 inch oval (larger target than heart alone)
- **Result**: Rapid oxygen loss, death in 30-60 seconds
- **Tracking**: 25-100 yards
- **Shot angle**: Broadside or slight quartering

**3. Spine (High Risk/High Reward)**
- **Location**: Directly above heart/lung line, centerline of back
- **Size**: 2-3 inches wide (small target)
- **Result**: Instant paralysis and death (if hit), no effect if missed by inches
- **Tracking**: Drops immediately or no effect
- **Shot angle**: Broadside only (clear spine shot)
- **Risk**: Easy to miss, may wound without killing

**4. Brain (Expert Only)**
- **Location**: Between eyes, slightly above eye line, 2-4 inches back from face
- **Size**: 3-5 inches (softball size)
- **Result**: Instant death (if hit), no effect if missed
- **Tracking**: Drops immediately or unharmed
- **Risk**: Small target, skull deflects some bullets, animal movement
- **Use**: Close range (<50 yards), finishing wounded animal

**5. Neck (Not Recommended)**
- **Location**: Center of neck, midway between head and chest
- **Size**: 4-6 inches (vertical target)
- **Result**: Severs spine or major arteries (instant death) OR wounds trachea/esophagus (slow death)
- **Risk**: High miss rate, unpredictable results
- **Avoid**: Use heart/lung shot instead

## ü¶å Species-Specific Anatomy

### Moose (*Alces alces*)

**Size**: 800-1200 lbs (bulls), 600-900 lbs (cows)  
**Vitals Location**: Large chest cavity, but set farther back than deer

**Shot Placement:**
- **Broadside**: Aim 1/3 up from chest bottom, center of shoulder blade (or just behind)
- **Quartering Away**: Aim for opposite shoulder (bullet travels diagonally through lungs)
- **Target Zone**: 12-15 inch diameter (large but aim small)

**Challenges:**
- Massive chest cavity (can absorb poorly placed shots)
- Heavy bone structure (use adequate caliber)
- May travel 50-100 yards even with perfect heart shot (momentum)

**Avoid**:
- Quartering toward (shoulder bone blocks vitals)
- Head-on (too much bone, no vital exposure)
- Rear shots (gut shot = slow, painful death)

### Caribou (*Rangifer tarandus*)

**Size**: 200-400 lbs (bulls), 150-300 lbs (cows)  
**Vitals Location**: Similar to deer, but slightly larger chest cavity

**Shot Placement:**
- **Broadside**: Aim behind shoulder, lower 1/3 of body
- **Target Zone**: 8-10 inch diameter

**Challenges:**
- Often moving (migratory behavior)
- Wind affects bullet trajectory (open tundra)
- Herd dynamics (hit the right animal)

**Advantages:**
- Lighter bone structure (easier penetration)
- Typically drop quickly with good shot

### White-Tailed / Mule Deer (*Odocoileus* species)

**Size**: 100-250 lbs (bucks), 80-150 lbs (does)  
**Vitals Location**: Standard for reference (other game compared to deer)

**Shot Placement:**
- **Broadside**: Behind front leg, lower 1/3 of body
- **Target Zone**: 8 inch diameter

**Challenges:**
- Smaller target (less room for error)
- Often in brush (branches deflect bullets)
- Nervous, alert (sudden movement)

**Advantages:**
- Lighter animal (drops faster with good shot)
- Abundant (more practice opportunities)

## üìê Shot Angles & Aiming Points

### Broadside (BEST)

**Animal Orientation**: 90¬∞ to shooter, side fully exposed

**Aiming Point:**
- Visualize vertical line behind front leg (shoulder blade)
- Aim 1/3 up from bottom of chest
- Center of "crease" behind shoulder

**Why Best:**
- Largest vital zone exposure
- No bones blocking vitals (if just behind shoulder)
- Clear view of animal's posture
- Easiest range estimation

**Shot Result**: Heart and/or both lungs, rapid death, short tracking

### Quartering Away (GOOD)

**Animal Orientation**: 45¬∞ angle, rear quarters toward shooter

**Aiming Point:**
- Aim for FAR SHOULDER (opposite side)
- Bullet travels diagonally through chest cavity
- Slightly farther back than broadside (to compensate for angle)

**Why Good:**
- Bullet path through vitals is long (slices through heart/lungs)
- Less likely to hit shoulder bone
- Animal often unaware (not looking at you)

**Shot Result**: Double lung, heart, rapid death

**Caution**: Don't shoot too far back (gut shot)

### Quartering Toward (POOR)

**Animal Orientation**: 45¬∞ angle, front quarters toward shooter

**Aiming Point:**
- Front of chest, where neck meets shoulder
- Aim to reach vitals behind opposite shoulder

**Why Poor:**
- Shoulder bone blocks bullet path
- Narrow vitals exposure
- Easy to miss vitals entirely (lung shot from front is shallow)

**Recommendation**: Wait for better angle or pass on shot

### Head-On (AVOID)

**Animal Orientation**: Facing shooter directly

**Why Avoid:**
- Thick chest/shoulder bones block vitals
- Difficult to hit heart/lungs from front
- High chance of gut shot (bullet travels length of body)
- Broken jaw or superficial wound (animal escapes)

**Exception**: Brain shot at very close range (<25 yards) if you're an expert

### Rear/Running Away (NEVER)

**Animal Orientation**: Rear toward shooter or moving away

**Why Never:**
- Gut shot (intestines, stomach) = slow, painful death
- No vital organs exposed
- Wounded animal may travel miles
- Illegal in most jurisdictions
- Unethical

**What to Do**: Let animal go, wait for better opportunity

## üî´ Caliber & Bullet Selection

### Recommended Calibers by Species

| Species | Minimum Caliber | Ideal Caliber | Bullet Weight |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Moose** | .30-06 | .300 Win Mag, .338 Win Mag | 180-220 grains |
| **Caribou** | .243 Win | .270 Win, .30-06 | 130-180 grains |
| **Deer** | .243 Win | .270 Win, .308 Win | 130-165 grains |

### Bullet Types

**Soft Point / Expanding:**
- Mushrooms on impact (larger wound channel)
- Transfers energy to tissue (rapid death)
- Recommended for most hunting

**Full Metal Jacket (FMJ):**
- Passes through without expanding
- Less tissue damage (slower death)
- NOT recommended for hunting (use for target practice only)

**Bonded / Controlled Expansion:**
- Expands but retains weight (deep penetration)
- Good for heavy bone, large game
- Premium option (more expensive)

### Energy Requirements

**Minimum Kinetic Energy at Impact:**
- Moose: 2000 ft-lbs
- Caribou: 1200 ft-lbs
- Deer: 1000 ft-lbs

**Check Your Rifle**: Use ballistics calculator (online) to verify energy at your shooting distance

## üéØ Range Considerations

### Ethical Shot Distances

| Skill Level | Max Range (Broadside) | Notes |
|-------------|-----------------------|-------|
| Beginner | 100 yards | Learn fundamentals first |
| Intermediate | 200 yards | Confident in rifle, conditions |
| Advanced | 300+ yards | Wind reading, ballistics knowledge |

**Factors Affecting Max Range:**
- Shooter skill (can you hit 6-inch circle at this range?)
- Rifle accuracy (sub-MOA preferred)
- Conditions (wind, rain, light)
- Shooting position (prone > sitting > kneeling > standing)
- Animal behavior (calm vs alert)

**Golden Rule**: If you're not confident you can hit a 6-inch circle at this range, don't take the shot.

### Zero Distance

**Recommended Zeros:**
- 200 yards: Good all-around (¬±3 inches from 50-250 yards)
- 100 yards: Short range hunting (thick brush)
- 300 yards: Long range (open tundra, mountains)

**Practice**: Zero rifle before hunting season, verify at range

## ü©∏ Tracking & Recovery

### Blood Trail Reading

**Good Hit (Heart/Lung):**
- Bright red, frothy blood (lung shot) or dark red (heart shot)
- Heavy blood trail
- Animal runs 10-100 yards, leaves trail
- Found dead within short distance

**Poor Hit (Gut/Liver):**
- Dark, watery blood or stomach contents
- Sparse blood trail
- Animal may travel 0.5+ miles
- Found bedded down, still alive (dangerous)

**Muscle Hit (Non-Vital):**
- Dark red blood, small amounts
- Long trail (animal may survive)
- Difficult recovery

### Tracking Protocol

**Immediately After Shot:**
1. **Note where animal was standing** (mark with hat, flagging tape)
2. **Observe animal's reaction** (instant drop = good hit, running = assess)
3. **Wait 30-60 minutes** (don't push wounded animal, let it bed down)
4. **Mark blood trail** (flagging tape every 10-20 yards)
5. **Follow slowly, quietly** (don't spook animal if bedded)

**If Blood Trail Disappears:**
1. Mark last blood location
2. Search in expanding circles (10-20 yard radius)
3. Look for disturbed ground, broken branches
4. Check thick cover, water sources (animals seek shelter/water)

**If Animal Beds Down:**
- Approach from downwind (scent awareness)
- Have rifle ready (may need finishing shot)
- Be prepared for charge (moose can be dangerous when wounded)

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

### ‚ùå Shooting Too Far
**Problem**: Overestimate skill, wind, or animal movement  
**Result**: Wounded animal, no recovery  
**Fix**: Know your limits, practice at range, pass on long shots

### ‚ùå Rushing the Shot
**Problem**: Animal about to leave, panic shot  
**Result**: Poor placement, gut shot  
**Fix**: Breathe, aim carefully, or let animal go

### ‚ùå Not Accounting for Angle
**Problem**: Shoot same spot on quartering animal as broadside  
**Result**: Miss vitals, hit guts or shoulder bone  
**Fix**: Visualize bullet path to far-side vitals

### ‚ùå Shooting at Moving Animal
**Problem**: Lead is off, bullet hits rear or front  
**Result**: Gut shot or miss  
**Fix**: Wait for animal to stop, or practice moving shots extensively

### ‚ùå Poor Follow-Up
**Problem**: Assume animal is dead, don't track, push too fast  
**Result**: Wounded animal escapes  
**Fix**: Always wait 30-60 min, track carefully, be patient

## üõ°Ô∏è Safety & Ethics

### Know Your Backdrop
- Never shoot unless you can see what's behind the animal
- Bullets travel 2+ miles (depending on caliber)
- Ricochets off rocks, water

### One Shot, One Kill Philosophy
- Take only shots you're 90%+ confident in
- Practice in off-season (100+ rounds minimum)
- Know your rifle's accuracy (shoot groups at range)

### Finishing Shots
- If animal is down but alive, approach carefully
- Shoot again (brain or heart) to end suffering
- Don't let animal struggle

### Respect the Animal
- Use all edible meat (don't waste)
- Quick, clean kill minimizes suffering
- Thank the animal (many indigenous traditions)

## üìã Pre-Shot Checklist

**Before pulling trigger:**
- [ ] I can clearly see vital zone (not obscured by brush)
- [ ] Range is within my skill limit
- [ ] Animal is in acceptable shooting position (broadside or quartering away)
- [ ] I have a stable shooting position (rest, sling, bipod)
- [ ] I know what's beyond the animal (safe backdrop)
- [ ] I am calm and breathing steadily
- [ ] Crosshairs are on aiming point (1/3 up from chest, behind shoulder)
