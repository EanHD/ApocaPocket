# Northeast US Regional Survival Guide

**Region Coverage:** Maine, New Hampshire, Vermont, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Maryland, Delaware, West Virginia  
**USDA Hardiness Zones:** 3b-7b  
**Last Updated:** 2026-02  
**Authoritative Sources:** NOAA National Weather Service, USGS, State Fish & Wildlife Agencies

## Climate Profile

### Temperature Ranges (Humid Continental)
| Season | Avg High | Avg Low | Extreme High | Extreme Low |
|--------|----------|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Summer (Jun-Aug) | 70-85°F | 55-65°F | 100°F+ | 45°F |
| Fall (Sep-Nov) | 50-70°F | 35-50°F | 85°F | 0°F |
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | 20-40°F | 0-25°F | 60°F | -40°F |
| Spring (Mar-May) | 45-65°F | 30-45°F | 85°F | -5°F |

**Regional Variation:**
- **Coastal (MA, RI, CT, NJ):** Moderated by ocean, milder winters, cooler summers
- **Mountains (Appalachians, Adirondacks, White/Green Mountains):** Colder, more snow, severe weather
- **Interior (NY, PA, northern regions):** True continental climate, extreme temperature swings

### Precipitation & Snow
- **Annual rainfall:** 35-50 inches (evenly distributed)
- **Annual snowfall:** 40-120 inches (higher elevations: 200+ inches)
- **Lake-effect snow:** Western NY, PA (Great Lakes influence) - 100-200+ inches
- **Nor'easters:** October-April (coastal storms, heavy snow + wind + flooding)

### Critical Weather Patterns

**Nor'easters (October-April):**
- Multi-day winter storms (coastal low pressure systems)
- Heavy snow (1-3 feet common, up to 4+ feet)
- High winds (40-70 mph, coastal 80+ mph)
- Coastal flooding (storm surge + high tides)
- Power outages (days to weeks)

**Ice Storms (December-March):**
- Freezing rain coats everything (trees, power lines, roads)
- 1/4 to 2+ inches of ice accumulation
- Tree/power line collapse (widespread, prolonged outages)
- Roads impassable (ice layer on pavement)

**Lake-Effect Snow (November-February):**
- Cold air over warm Great Lakes = continuous snow
- Localized (narrow bands, 10-20 miles wide)
- Intense (2-4 inches per hour, multi-day duration)
- Totals: 3-6 feet in single event possible

**Cold Snaps (January-February):**
- Arctic air outbreaks (-20°F to -40°F, especially mountains/northern)
- Wind chill -30°F to -50°F
- Multi-day duration (3-7 days)

## Biomes & Terrain

### Deciduous/Mixed Forests (Dominant Biome)
- **Features:** Rolling hills, dense tree cover, rocky terrain
- **Vegetation:** Oak, maple, birch, beech, hemlock, pine
- **Water:** Abundant streams, springs, lakes
- **Advantages:** Shelter materials, firewood, nuts, game, sugar maple (syrup)
- **Hazards:** Black bears (increasing), Lyme disease (endemic), rugged terrain

### Appalachian Mountains (ME to WV)
- **Features:** Ancient mountains (2000-6000 ft), steep valleys, rocky ridges
- **Vegetation:** Mixed hardwoods (low), spruce-fir (high), alpine zone (highest peaks)
- **Water:** Mountain streams (cold, clean), snowmelt-fed
- **Advantages:** Elevation = cooler summers, reliable water, game
- **Hazards:** Severe weather, hypothermia (rapid weather changes), isolation, black bears

### Atlantic Coast (ME to MD)
- **Features:** Rocky coast (north), sandy beaches (south), tidal zones, salt marshes
- **Vegetation:** Pitch pine, scrub oak, beach grasses, salt-tolerant species
- **Water:** Brackish estuaries, freshwater limited in coastal areas
- **Advantages:** Seafood (fish, shellfish, seaweed), moderate temperatures
- **Hazards:** Nor'easters (flooding, wind), hurricanes (rare but possible), cold water (hypothermia)

### Great Lakes Plains (Western NY, PA)
- **Features:** Flat-to-rolling, agricultural land, lake shores
- **Vegetation:** Deciduous forests, grasslands (cleared for farming)
- **Water:** Great Lakes (Erie, Ontario), rivers
- **Advantages:** Fishing (Great Lakes), moderate summer temps
- **Hazards:** Lake-effect snow (extreme), cold winters

### Large Game

| Animal | Weight | Season | Habitat | Hunting Notes | Yield (lbs meat) |
|--------|--------|--------|---------|---------------|------------------|
| White-tailed deer | 100-250 lbs | Nov-Dec | Edge habitat, woods | Most important game, abundant | 40-100 |
| Black bear | 150-400 lbs | Sep-Nov (varies) | Forests, mountains | Dangerous, fat harvested, excellent meat | 75-200 |

**White-tailed Deer:** PRIMARY GAME
- 3+ million in Northeast region
- Adapt to suburbs, farmland, deep forest
- November rut (mating) = bucks less cautious
- All parts usable: Meat, hide (clothing, shelter), sinew (cordage), bones/antlers (tools)

**Black Bear:** Increasingly common (rebound from near-extinction)
- Found in all wooded areas (even suburbs in some states)
- Hibernate Nov-Mar (den in hollow trees, caves, brush piles)
- Fall fattening (pre-hibernation) = best eating (fat layer)
- Render fat = cooking oil, preservation

**Moose:** Northern only (ME, NH, VT, northern NY)
- Largest game animal in Northeast (bulls up to 1400 lbs)
- Solitary, wetland/forest interface
- **Dangerous:** Aggressive during rut (Sep-Oct), cows with calves (May-Jul)
- One moose = 400-600 lbs meat (feeds family for months)

## Water Sources & Purification

### Water Availability (Abundant Year-Round)

**Spring (March-May):**
- **Very abundant:** Snowmelt, spring rains
- **Quality:** Excellent (cold, runoff from snow)
- **Concern:** Spring flooding (turbid water), agricultural runoff
- **Streams:** High flow, fast current

**Summer (June-August):**
- **Abundant:** Rivers, lakes, streams, springs
- **Quality:** Good (cooler than southern regions)
- **Concern:** Giardia/Cryptosporidium (common), algae blooms (warm weather)
- **Springs:** Best source (cold, clean)

**Fall (September-November):**
- **Abundant:** Seasonal rains, streams flowing
- **Quality:** Excellent (cooling temperatures)
- **Concern:** Leaf tannins (harmless but discolors water brown)

**Winter (December-February):**
- **Frozen:** Most surface water ice-covered (6-36 inches thick)
- **Quality:** Excellent (no bacterial growth in ice)
- **Concern:** Energy cost to melt (BTU intensive)
- **Flowing water:** Larger rivers/streams flow under ice, springs don't freeze

### Source Types

**Mountain Streams:**
- Cold (40-55°F even in summer)
- Fast-flowing (well-oxygenated)
- Rocky substrate (natural filtration)
- **Best quality** (but still purify - Giardia present)

**Springs:**
- Year-round liquid water (even in winter)
- Emerges from ground (pre-filtered)
- Cold (45-50°F)
- **Excellent source** (still purify to be safe)

**Lakes & Ponds:**
- Freeze solid in winter (12-36" ice)
- Summer: Warmer, algae possible
- Beaver activity = Giardia risk (common)

**Rivers:**
- Large rivers (Hudson, Susquehanna, Connecticut, Delaware) = pollution concern near cities
- Small rivers/streams = good
- Winter: Flow under ice (ice fishing holes)

### Winter Water Access

**Method 1: Ice Hole**
- Chop/drill through ice (6-36 inches thick)
- Use auger, axe, or chisel
- Keep hole open (re-clear daily, ice reforms overnight)
- Cover hole when not in use (safety, reduces refreezing)

**Method 2: Spring**
- Springs don't freeze (ground temp ~50°F)
- Look for:
  - Green vegetation in winter (fed by spring)
  - Open water when everything else frozen
  - Hillsides, valley bottoms

**Method 3: Melt Ice/Snow**
- 10:1 ratio (10 cups snow = 1 cup water)
- Clear ice from lakes = more efficient (less air, more water)
- **NEVER EAT SNOW DIRECTLY** (hypothermia, energy loss)
- Requires significant fuel/firewood

### Purification
**Priority:**
1. **Boil:** 1 minute rolling boil (3 minutes above 5000 ft)
2. **Filter:** 0.2-micron (removes Giardia, Cryptosporidium, bacteria)
3. **Chemical:** Iodine/chlorine (less effective in cold water, need longer contact time)

**Giardia:** Endemic in Northeast (beaver fever), causes severe diarrhea
- Transmitted by beaver, muskrat, and other mammals
- Present in most backcountry water sources
- Boiling and filtration both effective

**DIAGRAM SPEC:** Winter water access methods (ice hole, spring identification), water purification method selection flowchart.

## Shelter Construction

### Climate Requirements

**Winter Shelter (Priority):**
- **Extreme insulation:** R-40 to R-60 (colder than Midwest in mountains)
- **Heat retention:** Thick walls, small interior space
- **Snow load:** Roof must support 3-5+ feet snow (50-80 lbs per sq ft)
- **Wind protection:** North/northwest side especially
- **Ventilation:** CRITICAL (carbon monoxide from heating)
- **Moisture control:** Snow melts on warm roof, creates moisture

**Summer Shelter:**
- Moderate insulation (cool nights even in summer, 50-65°F)
- Rain protection (frequent rain year-round)
- Ventilation (humid summers)
- Insect protection (mosquitoes, black flies)

### Winter Shelter Types

#### Permanent Cabin (Long-term Survival)
```
Design: Heavily insulated, small, efficient
- Walls: Log (12-18" diameter), chinked with moss/mud, or timber frame with thick insulation
- Insulation: R-40+ (double walls with hay/leaf/moss fill, or modern insulation if available)
- Roof: Steep pitch (45°+, snow slides off), support 80+ lbs per sq ft
- Size: SMALL = easier to heat (10x12 ft for 1-2 people)
- Heat source: Wood stove OR stone fireplace (CRITICAL)
- Chimney: Well-drafted, spark arrestor, regular cleaning
- Floor: Raised or insulated (ground cold sinks heat)
- Windows: Small, south-facing (solar gain), shuttered at night (insulated shutters)
- Door: Insulated, weather-stripped, consider double-door entry (airlock)
```

**Firewood Requirement:** 4-6 cords per winter (more in mountains)

**DIAGRAM SPEC:** Cross-section of insulated cabin showing wall construction, heat source, ventilation, snow load on roof.

#### Snow Cave (Emergency, Mountains)
```
Design: Excavated into snowbank/drift
- Find deep snowdrift (6+ feet, lee side of ridge/hill)
- Tunnel horizontally 3-4 feet
- Excavate chamber (6x8 ft, rounded ceiling)
- Sleeping platform raised 12-18" above entrance
- Ventilation hole through roof (CRITICAL)
- Block entrance with snow blocks (leave gap at bottom)
- Interior temp: 20-40°F (vs -20°F outside)
```

**Time:** 2-4 hours to build  
**Duration:** Days to weeks (with maintenance)  
**Advantage:** Excellent insulation, protects from wind

#### Lean-to + Fire Reflector (Short-term)
```
Design: Open shelter with reflector fire
- Back wall: Logs, bark, boughs (windbreak)
- Roof: Angled 45°, bark or boughs (3-4 layers deep)
- Floor: Boughs, leaves (insulation from ground)
- Fire: 4-6 feet in front of shelter
- Reflector wall: Behind fire (logs, rocks), reflects heat into shelter
- Open front: Allows heat in, smoke escapes
```

**Temperature:** Comfortable in 20-40°F, survival in colder  
**Limitation:** Must tend fire all night (person awake)

### Summer Shelter (Simpler)
```
Design: Rain protection, ventilation, insect barriers
- Framework: Branches, saplings
- Roof: Bark (birch, tulip poplar), boughs, leaves (thick layer)
- Walls: Partial (wind protection, not full enclosure)
- Floor: Elevated 6-12" (ground moisture, insects) OR thick leaf/bough bed
- Screening: Fine netting or smoke (mosquitoes, black flies)
```

### Insulation Materials (Winter)

**Best to Worst:**
1. **Dry leaves (oak, beech):** R-1.5 per inch, abundant, pack tightly
2. **Pine needles:** R-1 per inch, aromatic, good drainage
3. **Dry grass/hay:** R-1.5 per inch (if available)
4. **Boughs (spruce, fir):** R-0.5 per inch, but good layering
5. **Moss:** R-0.5 per inch, but holds moisture (dry thoroughly)
6. **Snow:** R-1 per inch (igloo principle, once structure is built)

**Critical:** Must be DRY. Wet = no insulation + hypothermia.

**DIAGRAM SPEC:** Lean-to + reflector fire setup, snow cave construction steps, insulation layering technique.

## Fire Making & Management

### Wood Selection (BTU & Characteristics)

| Wood Type | BTU/cord | Burn Rate | Coals | Split Difficulty | Availability |
|-----------|----------|-----------|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| Oak (red, white) | 24-30M | Slow | Excellent | Hard | Very common |
| Sugar maple | 24-27M | Moderate-slow | Excellent | Moderate | Very common |
| Birch (yellow) | 21-24M | Moderate | Very good | Moderate | Common |
| Ash | 23-25M | Moderate | Very good | Easy | Common |
| Beech | 24-27M | Slow | Excellent | Moderate | Common |
| Hickory | 28-30M | Slow | Excellent | Hard | Less common |
| Cherry | 20-21M | Moderate | Good | Easy | Common |
| Pine (white) | 15-17M | Fast | Poor | Easy | Very common |
| Hemlock | 15-16M | Fast | Poor | Easy | Common (mountains) |
| Poplar | 13-14M | Fast | Poor | Very easy | Common |

**Best all-around:** Sugar maple (high BTU, abundant, splits well, bonus = syrup in spring)  
**Best for heating:** Oak, beech (long, steady burn)  
**Best fire starting:** Birch bark (starts even when wet), pine (resinous)

### Birch Bark - Northeast Secret Weapon
**Unique Advantage:** Birch bark contains oils that burn even when soaking wet
- Paper birch, yellow birch, black birch (all have flammable bark)
- Harvest from dead/fallen trees (taking from live tree kills it)
- Peel in sheets (paper-thin layers)
- **Best fire starter in Northeast** (better than fatwood)

### Winter Fire Strategy

**Heat Requirements:**
- Space heating: 15,000-40,000 BTU/hour (depending on shelter size, insulation, outside temp)
- Wood consumption: 4-6 cords per winter (Nov-Apr, 6 months)
- Mountain/northern regions: Up to 8 cords

**Daily Routine (Winter):**
```
5-6 AM: Stoke fire from coals (add birch bark, kindling, small splits)
7 AM: Load with hardwood splits (3-4 hours burn)
11 AM: Reload
3 PM: Reload
7 PM: Bank fire for overnight (large splits, tight packing, damper down)
Overnight: Smoldering coals (restart in morning)
```

**Banking Fire (Overnight):**
1. Build up thick coal bed (1-2 hours of hot fire)
2. Add largest hardwood splits (6-10 inch diameter)
3. Pack tightly (reduces oxygen = slower burn)
4. Damper down (but NOT completely - need some draft)
5. Result: Fire burns slow, leaves coals in morning (easy restart)

### Fire Safety

**Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning:**
- **SILENT KILLER:** Odorless, colorless, kills while you sleep
- Symptoms: Headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, death
- **Prevention:**
  - Always have chimney/vent (draft pulls smoke out)
  - Crack window or dedicated air intake (fresh air in)
  - Never seal shelter completely
  - If headache develops, get outside immediately

**Chimney Fire:**
- Creosote buildup (from incomplete combustion, softwood smoke)
- Ignites suddenly (roaring sound like freight train or jet)
- Can reach 2000°F, spread to structure
- **Prevention:**
  - Burn hot fires (not smoldering)
  - Use dry hardwood (less creosote)
  - Clean chimney regularly (inspect monthly, clean if 1/4" buildup)

**Structure Fire:**
- Clearance: 3 feet from combustibles
- Floor protection: Stone, brick, metal under stove
- Spark screen on open fireplaces
- Check for sparks on roof (especially cedar shake, thatch)

**DIAGRAM SPEC:** Fire lay types (teepee, log cabin, long-burn), birch bark harvesting and use, proper chimney installation.

## Seasonal Survival Strategies

### Winter (December-February) - SURVIVAL MODE

**Challenges:**
- **Extreme cold:** -10°F to -40°F (mountains/northern), wind chill -50°F
- **Short days:** 8-9 hours daylight (northern regions even less)
- **Heavy snow:** 6-12 feet accumulated, nor'easters add 1-3 feet at a time
- **Frozen water:** All surface water ice-covered (ice holes or melt snow)
- **Limited food:** No plants, game scarce (deep snow, hibernation)
- **Isolation:** Storms make travel impossible for days/weeks
- **Power outages:** Nor'easters/ice storms = days to weeks without power

**Priorities:**
1. **FIRE MAINTENANCE:** 24/7 heat (life or death)
2. **Firewood:** Burn 1/2 to 1+ cords per month (keep 2 weeks ahead indoors)
3. **Water:** Maintain ice hole or melt snow (1 gal per day per person)
4. **Food rationing:** Stretch fall harvest (nuts, dried meat, smoked fish)
5. **Ice fishing:** Critical winter protein (perch, pickerel, trout)
6. **Shelter maintenance:** Clear snow from roof (collapse risk at 3+ feet), maintain insulation
7. **Mental health:** Long, dark, isolated (boredom, depression risk)

**Daily Routine:**
```
Dawn (7-8 AM): Stoke fire, melt snow for water, eat breakfast
Morning: Check ice fishing lines, collect firewood, clear snow
Midday: Meal, fire maintenance, equipment repair, indoor work
Afternoon: Ice fishing, hunting if clear, firewood processing
Evening (4-5 PM): Already dark, banking fire, meal, early sleep
```

**Survival Rules:**
- Stay DRY (wet = hypothermia = death)
- Keep fire going (losing fire in -20°F = life-threatening emergency)
- Ration food (spring is 3-4 months away)
- Don't overexert (sweating = wet = hypothermia)
- **NEVER travel during nor'easter** (whiteout, disorientation, death)

### Spring (March-May) - AWAKENING

**Advantages:**
- **Maple sugaring:** Feb-Mar (syrup production, morale boost)
- Temperatures moderating (30-60°F)
- Days lengthening (9 → 13+ hours daylight)
- **Spring greens:** Fiddleheads, ramps, dandelion (vitamin C, morale)
- Fish spawning (trout streams, excellent fishing)
- Waterfowl migration (ducks, geese)
- Turkey season (April-May, most states)
- **END OF WINTER ISOLATION**

**Challenges:**
- Mud season (freeze/thaw cycles, impassable roads/trails)
- Late snowstorms (nor'easters possible through April)
- Flooding (snowmelt + rain, rivers overbank)
- Avalanche risk (mountains, warming temperatures destabilize snowpack)
- Ice-out (lakes dangerous, thin ice)

**Priorities:**
- **Maple syrup production:** Tap trees in February, collect sap, boil down (40:1 ratio)
- Repair shelter from winter damage (roof, walls, chimney)
- Restock firewood (while still cold, before bugs)
- Forage spring greens (nutrition, variety)
- Fish spawning runs (stock protein for summer)
- Plant garden (if long-term, late April-May)

### Summer (June-August) - ABUNDANCE

**Advantages:**
- Long days (15+ hours daylight)
- Warm (70-80°F, comfortable)
- **Berry season:** June-August (strawberry, raspberry, blackberry, blueberry)
- Fishing excellent (warm water, active fish)
- Growing season (gardens produce)
- Travel easy (dry ground, no snow)
- Minimal heating needs (cool nights, but not freezing)

**Challenges:**
- **Black flies:** May-June (Northeast plague, drive humans insane)
- **Mosquitoes:** June-August (wetlands, woods)
- **Ticks:** May-September (Lyme disease endemic)
- Severe thunderstorms (rare but possible)
- Humid (less than Midwest/Southeast, but present)

**Priorities:**
- **Berry harvest:** Dry, jam, eat fresh (vitamin C, calories)
- Fishing (smoke/dry for winter)
- Hunting (if seasons open, but most closed in summer)
- Build/improve shelter (before winter)
- Firewood collection (cut now, season for winter)
- Gather insulation materials (leaves, moss - must dry thoroughly)
- **Tick checks daily** (Lyme disease)

### Fall (September-November) - HARVEST & PREP

**Advantages:**
- **HARVEST SEASON:** Acorns, hickory, beechnuts (critical winter calories)
- Cooling temperatures (50-70°F → 30-50°F)
- Beautiful weather (clear, crisp)
- Deer hunting (Nov-Dec, most states)
- Waterfowl hunting (migration)
- Fish active before winter
- Apple harvest (wild/feral trees)

**Challenges:**
- Rapidly shortening days (13 → 9 hours)
- First frost (kills plants, ends foraging)
- Early nor'easters (November, first snow)
- Hypothermia risk (people not prepared for cold yet)
- **Deadline pressure:** Winter coming, must be ready

**Priorities:**
1. **NUT COLLECTION (CRITICAL):** Acorns, hickory, beechnuts (primary winter calories)
2. **Deer hunting:** Late season (winter meat supply)
3. **Firewood:** MINIMUM 5 cords, more if possible (cut/split/stack before snow)
4. **Shelter winterization:**
   - Insulate walls (pack leaves/moss in double walls)
   - Chink gaps (moss, mud, modern caulk if available)
   - Roof repair (before snow load)
   - Snow load reinforcement (extra roof supports if needed)
5. **Smoke/dry meat:** Preservation for winter
6. **Move food to cool storage:** Root cellar, outdoor cache (natural refrigeration)

**Rule of Thumb:** If not ready for winter by November 1, you're behind. First nor'easter can hit mid-November.

## Regional Hazards Summary

**Primary Killers:**
1. **Hypothermia/cold exposure:** Winter (-20°F to -40°F in mountains/north)
2. **Nor'easters:** Multi-day snowstorms, coastal flooding, power outages (days-weeks)
3. **Ice storms:** Power line collapse, tree damage, roads impassable
4. **Black bear encounters:** Increasing population, food aggression (rare attacks)
5. **Lyme disease:** Endemic (highest rates in US), tick exposure May-Sep

**See l2-regional-northeast-hazards.md for detailed hazard protocols.**
